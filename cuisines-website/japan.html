Brief History of Japanese Cuisine

Like the nation itself, Japanese food has a rich and diverse past. It originated during the Jomon period (14,000–300 BCE), when vegetables, fish, and rice made up the majority of the diet. Since Buddhism discourages eating meat, there has been a noticeable movement towards vegetarian food since its arrival in the sixth century CE. Sushi and other now-iconic fish dishes were created during the Edo period (1603–1867 CE), which revolutionized Japanese cuisine. Japanese cuisine has adopted Western culinary influences in the modern era, adding new ingredients and cooking methods while maintaining its traditional flavor. The Japanese diet is still characterized by this blending of the traditional and the modern.

Sushi: A Celebration of Japanese Culinary Art

The most well-known Japanese cuisine outside of Japan is sushi (寿司 or 鮨), which is also one of the most well-liked Japanese foods. Sushi is typically consumed in Japan at celebrations and other special events.

Pickled fish preserved in vinegar was referred to as "sushi" in earlier times. In modern times, sushi is a rice dish that has been made with sushi vinegar. Sushi comes in a variety of forms. Several well-known ones are listed here.

Culinary Districts Across Japan

Tokyo

Ginza - Tokyo's most well-known upscale shopping, dining, and entertainment area is the Ginza (銀座), which is home to a large number of department shops, boutiques, art galleries, eateries, nightclubs, and cafes. The district's heart is home to some of Japan's most costly real estate, with a square meter of land valued at over 10 million yen. Fine dining establishments can be found in department shops and buildings off the main streets, despite the lack of skyscrapers.

Kyoto

Gion – Kyoto's Gion district is one of Japan's most iconic historic areas, renowned for traditional teahouses, cobblestone streets, and a rich culinary scene that brings centuries-old kaiseki dining to modern palates. Fine dining establishments are tucked along narrow lanes and in traditional wooden machiya houses, with some of the country's most renowned kaiseki restaurants nestled amid the old-world charm.

Osaka

Dontonbori district - The ideal location to immerse yourself in Osaka's culinary scene is the brightly lit Dotonbori area close to Namba Station, filled with various restaurants along its main road and numerous side alleys. The most well-known tourist site in Osaka is known for its ostentatious neon lights, opulent signage, and vast array of eateries and bars.

Hiroshima Food Guide

Okonomiyaki

The most renowned dish in Hiroshima is its unique variation of okonomiyaki. The regional variation features merely a light coating of batter and an ample portion of cabbage atop yakisoba noodles. Common toppings are oysters, squid, and cheese. The dish is finished with bonito flakes, green seaweed, and okonomiyaki sauce. Numerous okonomiyaki eateries can be located in Okonomimura and near Hiroshima Station.

Oysters

Hiroshima Prefecture contributes around 60-70% of Japan's total oyster output. Local oysters are eaten both locally and exported abroad. Oysters are available at various dining venues in Hiroshima and Miyajima, including restaurants, food stalls, and ryokan. They are consumed uncooked with soy sauce or ponzu, deep-fried, baked, grilled, or placed over rice (donburi).

Tsukemen

Hiroshima-style tsukemen consist of chilled noodles that are dipped into a spicy red pepper sauce prior to consumption, and they are recognized for their heat. Customers typically have the option to choose the level of spiciness on a scale from one to five. The noodles are consumed with a range of ingredients including sliced meat, eggs, shredded leeks, cucumbers, and cabbage.

Matcha

Finely ground dried tea leaves are used to make this Japanese green tea powder. The high quantities of chlorophyll in the leaves give it a vivid green hue and a slightly bitter, vegetal taste. Although it has long been a staple of traditional Japanese tea rituals, its health benefits have recently led to its popularity in the US. The samurai class of Japan embraced ceremonial tea drinking with great enthusiasm. The samurai were formidable fighters but also cultured and important figures in Japanese social hierarchy. The essence of the samurai was shaped by Zen Buddhism, incorporating values such as discipline, ceremony, and cleansing.

Fishing for Good Fortune

There will undoubtedly be shrines everywhere you go in Japan. Each one is devoted to a particular deity, such as the sea or the god of war. The water deity is Watatsumi. Additionally, fishermen go to Watatsumi shrines across the nation to donate fish as a way to seal the contract and pray for good fishing fate. It's a meditation on the close ties that Japanese people have with delicacies like salmon, which bridge the gap between the spiritual and material worlds.
